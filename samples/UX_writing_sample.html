01 / Context

The Problem with Generic Error Messages

Error messages are a critical but often overlooked part of the user experience. When a user hits an error, they are already frustrated. The message they see at that moment determines whether they resolve the issue independently or reach for the phone and file a support ticket.

Generic error messages, ones that describe the system state rather than the user's situation, consistently produce two outcomes: repeated page visits as users try to diagnose the problem themselves, and unnecessary escalations when they cannot. Both are preventable with better copy.

The core insight: A user who understands what went wrong and what to do next does not need to contact support. Error copy is not a system notification, it is the last line of self-service.

02 / Design Principles

What Good Error Copy Does

Before rewriting a single message, it helps to define what the error copy needs to accomplish. These three principles guided every rewrite in this sample.

🎯

Be specific about the cause

Name the actual problem. "Something went wrong" is not information. "Your card was declined because the billing address did not match" is.

🔧

Tell users what to do

Every error message should include a next step. If a user cannot act on the message, the message has not done its job.

🧭

Match the user's mental model

Use the language the user knows, not the language the system uses internally. Users think in tasks, not in error codes.

03 / The Rewrites

Before and After

The following three scenarios represent the most common error types in a payments and billing context. Each shows the original generic message and the redesigned version, with annotations explaining the rationale.

Scenario 1 — Payment failure
Before
⚠ Payment error
Your payment could not be processed. Please try again or contact support.
Try again
After
⚠ Payment declined by your bank
Your bank declined this payment. This is usually due to a spending limit or an expired card. Check your card details or try a different payment method.
Update payment method
What changed and why
  • Title: Names the cause ("declined by your bank") rather than the system event ("payment error")
  • Body: Gives the two most likely reasons so users can self-diagnose without contacting support
  • Action: Directs users to the specific fix rather than a generic retry that will fail again
Scenario 2 — Account budget limit reached
Before
⚠ Action unavailable
This action cannot be completed at this time. Your account has reached its limit.
OK
After
⚠ You have reached your account budget
Your ads are paused because your account budget has been reached for this period. Increase your budget to resume your campaigns.
Increase budget
What changed and why
  • Title: "Action unavailable" tells users nothing. The rewrite names the specific constraint
  • Body: Explains the consequence (ads paused) and the fix (increase budget) in one sentence
  • Action: "OK" closes the dialog without helping. The rewrite sends users directly to the resolution
Scenario 3 — Payment method verification failure
Before
⚠ Verification failed
We were unable to verify your payment information. Error code: VF-4021. Please contact support for assistance.
Contact support
After
⚠ We could not verify your billing address
The address you entered does not match your bank's records. Double-check your billing address or use a different card.
Edit billing address
What changed and why
  • Title: Replaces the system term "verification" with the user-facing cause — the billing address
  • Body: Removes the error code (meaningless to users) and explains the likely cause in plain language
  • Action: Routes users to the fix rather than immediately escalating to support
04 / Expected Outcomes

Why This Matters for Escalation Rates

Error messages that direct users toward a resolution keep them on a self-service journey. The table below maps each rewrite to its expected support deflection mechanism.

Scenario Root cause of escalation How the rewrite deflects it Expected impact
Payment failure Users do not know why payment failed and retry the same method repeatedly Names the cause and surfaces a direct path to update the payment method Fewer repeat contacts
Budget limit reached Users do not connect "action unavailable" to their budget setting, and contact support to understand why ads are paused Explicitly states the consequence and the fix in a single message Fewer "why are my ads paused" tickets
Verification failure Error codes and technical language prompt users to contact support rather than self-resolve Removes the error code, names the specific field causing the failure, and provides a direct action Higher self-resolution rate
Writer's Note

Decisions and trade-offs in this sample

This is a speculative redesign created to demonstrate UX writing principles in a payments and billing context. A few decisions worth noting:

  • Specificity over brevity. The rewritten messages are longer than the originals. That is intentional. In error states, users need enough information to act. Brevity that sacrifices clarity is not an improvement.
  • No error codes in user-facing copy. Error codes belong in logs and support tools, not in user interfaces. They prompt escalation rather than resolution. Where a code is unavoidable for support handoff purposes, it should be collapsed behind a "Details" link, not placed in the primary message.
  • Action label as the last word. The CTA is the last thing a user reads before deciding what to do. Each rewrite ensures the action label names the specific task, not a generic verb like "OK" or "Submit."
  • Tone. These messages use plain, direct language without being cold. The goal is to sound like a knowledgeable colleague explaining what happened, not a system notification.

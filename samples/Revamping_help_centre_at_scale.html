Case Study · Content Strategy

Revamping a Help Center at Scale

How I led a team of five writers through a structured, content-model-driven overhaul of 150 Help Center articles enforcing topic-based authoring, introducing country-variant content, and adding multimedia to improve clarity for a global user audience.

Role
Lead · Technical Writer Specialist
Scope
150 articles · external Help Center
Team
4 writers + Knowledge Manager
Note: All specific content, internal data, and proprietary workflows from this project are confidential. This case study describes my strategic process, frameworks, and decisions without disclosing any restricted information.
01 / Context

The Situation

As the content lead for a large-scale external Help Center, I owned the full content lifecycle of a critical support area spanning multiple global sites. The domain I covered was one of the most business-critical in the support ecosystem when users can't understand key processes, charges, or account status, they escalate to human support, delay tasks, or churn entirely.

I partnered with our Knowledge Manager to plan and execute a large-scale content revamp of the external, user-facing Help Center. Together we scoped the project, defined the content strategy, assigned articles across a team of five writers, and tracked delivery. The corpus covered 150 articles across multiple information types: task topics, concept topics, reference topics, troubleshooting articles, and country-specific articles.

The core challenge: 150 external articles with outdated UI references, multiple content types mixed within single articles, no visual aids, and readability levels mismatched to a global audience. One content lead, five writers, and a knowledge manager to fix all of it systematically.

02 / Problem Definition

What Was Breaking and Why

Before committing to a solution, I worked with the Knowledge Manager to audit a representative sample of articles and diagnose root causes. Four clear problems emerged:

🖥️

Outdated UI References

The product UI had evolved significantly. Many articles described navigation paths and screen labels that no longer existed, causing user confusion and unnecessary support escalations.

🗂️

Violation of Topic-Based Authoring

Task topics (procedural, step-by-step instructions) and concept topics (explanatory, reference content) were frequently combined in a single article. Users scanning for a task completion path had to navigate through conceptual background, and vice versa.

📖

Readability Gap

Articles were written at reading levels too high for a global audience many users were non-technical. Complex concepts needed to be simplified without losing accuracy.

🖼️

No Visual Aids

Complex flows and processes were explained in text only. Users with low English proficiency or low familiarity with the product had no visual reference to anchor understanding.

03 / Strategic Approach

Planning the Revamp with the Knowledge Manager

Before a single article was touched, I worked closely with the Knowledge Manager to establish three things: what we were fixing, in what order, and how we'd keep five writers aligned across both internal and external content simultaneously.

We agreed on a content strategy built around an established content model a framework that prescribes article types, structures, and voice guidelines. The strategic shift was to stop treating articles as free-form documents and start treating them as typed, predictable content objects. That decision drove everything downstream.

Key strategic decision: Enforce topic-based authoring across the corpus. Task topics (procedural content) and concept topics (reference and explanatory content) were separated into discrete articles. Each article was assigned a single information type, making content modular, reusable, and easier to maintain independently when the product UI changed.

For prioritisation, we used a two-signal framework article view volume and escalation correlation to rank which of the 150 articles to tackle first:

Signal What it tells us Priority
High traffic + high escalation Users are reading but not resolving broken content causing direct support cost P0 Immediate
High traffic + low escalation Content is working good candidate for UI refresh and multimedia uplift P1 Next cycle
Low traffic + high escalation Niche but broken investigate whether discoverability or content is the issue P1 Investigate
Low traffic + low escalation Low impact either way batch update in later phases P2 Batch

This framework let the Knowledge Manager and I divide the 150-article backlog into three delivery phases and assign them across the five writers with clear priorities rather than working through the list arbitrarily.

04 / Execution

The Revamp Process

Each article went through a structured five-step process. I set this process with the Knowledge Manager and applied it consistently across the team of five writers throughout the project.

01

Audit & Classification

Each article was audited for content type, UI accuracy, article structure, and terminology consistency across internal and external versions. Articles mixing how-to and troubleshooting content were flagged for splitting before rewriting began.

02

Structured Authoring and Content Model Application

Articles were restructured against the established content model a typed, standardised framework that prescribes structure by article type. How-to articles followed a consistent anatomy: task title, one-line summary, prerequisites, numbered steps with UI labels, and related links. Troubleshooting articles were separated and given their own structure: symptom, cause, resolution steps, escalation path.

03

UI Refresh & Terminology Alignment

All UI references were validated against the current product interface. Simultaneously, terminology was harmonised across internal and external articles so the same feature used the same name regardless of the reader. This was coordinated carefully to avoid introducing new inconsistencies during the rewrite.

04

Multimedia Collaboration

For complex flows and processes, I collaborated with the multimedia team to produce GIFs and videos. I briefed the visual requirements for each article, reviewed drafts for accuracy, and integrated the final assets. In total, 51 articles received new multimedia elements, driving significant rating improvements on key high-traffic articles.

04b

Country Selector Implementation

Rules and processes vary significantly by country. For 15 articles where content diverged meaningfully by geography, we implemented a country selector a dropdown that surfaces the relevant content for a user's country without requiring separate articles per locale. This reduced content duplication while improving relevance for users in markets with unique local rules.

05

Review, Sync & Publish

Articles went through peer review within the team before publish. The Knowledge Manager and I held regular syncs to track delivery against the phased plan, resolve blockers, and adjust prioritisation if product changes required urgent updates mid-cycle.

06 / Learnings

What I'd Do Differently

  • Document the content model spec before assigning articles. We applied the content model consistently, but a written spec shared with all five writers upfront would have reduced back-and-forth review cycles especially for edge-case articles that didn't fit cleanly into a single type.
  • Establish a terminology glossary as the first deliverable. Harmonising terminology across internal and external articles was one of the most valuable parts of this project but it happened iteratively. A glossary agreed on at the start would have made it faster and reduced the risk of individual writers using different terms for the same feature mid-project.
  • Build the multimedia pipeline earlier. Coordinating with the multimedia team on visual briefs worked well, but there was a lag between article completion and graphics delivery on some articles. Aligning multimedia production to the content delivery phases from the start would have reduced that gap.
